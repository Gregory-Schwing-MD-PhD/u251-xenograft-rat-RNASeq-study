\noindent To the Editor --- Laser interstitial thermal therapy (LITT) is increasingly deployed for deep-seated and recurrent glioblastoma, and profiling of the ablation margin has begun: single-cell studies have characterized the surrounding blood vessels, which transiently open the blood--brain barrier after ablation~\cite{Cleary}, and the immune microenvironment of the sublethal zone in a rodent model~\cite{Tao}. What the surviving tumor cells themselves do --- the transcriptional program of cells that persist in this zone and seed recurrence --- remains undefined.

We previously characterized a rat orthotopic U251N xenograft model of post-LITT recurrence --- MRI-guided ablation in athymic RNU/RNU rats --- by imaging, histology, and bulk RNA-seq of matched primary (pre-LITT) and recurrent (post-LITT) tumors~\cite{NagarajaJNS}. Here we reanalyze that dataset ($n=3$ per arm) using differential-expression and enrichment settings configured for small samples, extend it to in-silico drug repurposing, and release the underlying sequencing data publicly (GSE338105). Human tumor reads were resolved from rat host signal by xengsort cross-species read-sorting~\cite{xengsort}.

Primary and recurrent tumors separated clearly (Figure~\ref{fig1}A; PCA PC1 41.8\%, PC2 26.2\%), and the 35 differentially expressed genes (DESeq2; FDR\,$<$\,0.05, $|\log_{2}\mathrm{FC}|>1$; 23 up, 12 down) pointed to one dominant theme: the recurrent tumors had broadly shut down protein synthesis. Gene-set analysis configured for small samples ($n=3$ per arm) placed translation elongation (NES\,$=-1.96$), the EIF2AK4 (GCN2) response to amino-acid shortage (NES\,$=-1.95$), the ribosome (NES\,$=-1.94$), and the response to starvation (NES\,$=-1.93$) among the most reduced gene sets in recurrence (nominal $p<0.001$; FDR $q\approx0.15$--$0.25$) --- the pattern expected when the integrated stress response (ISR) suppresses translation. Subtype scores shifted in parallel: toward the Garofano Mitochondrial state ($z$ $-0.32\!\rightarrow\!+0.32$)~\cite{Garofano} and away from Neftel astrocyte-like ($+0.63\!\rightarrow\!-0.63$) and neural-progenitor-like ($+0.44\!\rightarrow\!-0.44$) programs~\cite{Neftel}, consistent with a stressed, metabolically rewired population of surviving cells. The most highly connected of these genes (\textit{BGN}, \textit{COL1A1}, \textit{IGFBP3}, \textit{CALB1}) further suggest remodeling of the extracellular matrix and of calcium signaling around them.

We then asked which existing drugs might oppose this state, screening the DSigDB drug-signature database and ranking candidates by a combined score: how strongly each drug's signature countered the tumor signature ($|\mathrm{NES}|^{1.5}$), weighted by its predicted ability to cross the blood--brain barrier. The leading candidates clustered on two mechanisms that fit a cell shutting down protein synthesis under metabolic stress (Figure~\ref{fig1}B): the HIF/iron axis --- the iron chelator and HIF-$1\alpha$ stabilizer ciclopirox (rank 1; score 3.4) and the prolyl-hydroxylase inhibitor dimethyloxalylglycine (rank 3; 3.0) --- and PI3K/mTOR, where the inhibitor LY-294002 ranked seventh (score 2.4). This is a roadmap, not a result: these are candidates for future preclinical testing alongside LITT, not validated treatments.

This study has clear limits. The sample is small ($n=3$ per arm), it uses a single cell line in one xenograft model, and we present no laboratory validation; the work is hypothesis-generating. Even so, we think the data merit early reporting: the size of the translation/ISR effect, the matching subtype shift, and the shared mechanisms among the nominated drugs are unlikely to be coincidental. As LITT moves further into first-line and salvage glioblastoma care, understanding what the surviving tumor cells are doing --- and designing drug combinations against them --- will be key to getting more from a procedure that is otherwise minimally invasive.
